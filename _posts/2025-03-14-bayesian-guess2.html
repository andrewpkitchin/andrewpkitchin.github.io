guessing with bayes

I'm thinking of a number between 1 and 100. Let's call the number n. Can you guess it?

Need a clue?

If you want a clue, click the button below. Each time you click, I’ll give you a number between 1 and n. You can use these clues to update your belief of how good your guess is. You can also go back and change your guess if you like.

The graph shows the probabilities of what n might be. This is updated each time you get a clue new clue. We start by assuming every number is equally likely - this is called a uniform prior. After each clue, we apply Bayes’ rule to update the distribution of probabilities.

Here's the details of what's going on.

Our prior belief is that all numbers are equally likely, so each number has a probability of 1% of actually being n. Now you get a clue, say c.

For each possible number i (from 1 to 100), you compute, what we call the likelihood, P(c | n = i). We read this as: the probability of getting c as the clue, given that n is equal to i. Let's look at the case when the first clue is 10.

  • If i < 10, then P(c | n = i) = 0, because the clue can't be greater than n.
  • If i ≥ 10, then P(c | n = i) = 1/i, because the clue is uniformly sampled between 1 and n.
Now multiply each prior by its corresponding likelihood. For example, for i = 20:
$$ P(n = 20 | c = 10) \propto P(c = 10 | n = 20) \cdot P(n = 20) = \frac{1}{20} \cdot \frac{1}{100} = 0.0005 $$ The symbol “∝” means “is proportional to.” That is, this isn’t the final probability yet — we still need to normalize these values so they add up to 1. After computing this product for every possible value of n, we divide each result by the total sum to get the final probabilities: $$ P(n = i | c = 10) = \frac{P(c = 10 | n = i) \cdot P(n = i)}{\sum_{j=1}^{100} P(c = 10 | n = j) \cdot P(n = j)} $$ This gives us the updated belief distribution — our new best guess for what the number might be.
